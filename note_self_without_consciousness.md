# Selfhood Without Consciousness

## A Note on Hegelian *Für-sich* as Logical Structure

## I. The Cartesian residue in contemporary philosophy of mind

The question *"can an artificial system be a self?"* inherits a structure from Descartes that most contemporary philosophers of mind share without noticing. The structure is this: a self is something that has a point of view; to have a point of view, it must have consciousness; therefore the question of selfhood reduces to the question of consciousness. On this picture, asking whether a large language model is a self and asking whether a large language model is conscious are the same question, and the second is governed by the "hard problem" — the question of whether there is something it is like to be the system. If the hard problem has no tractable answer for non-biological systems, then the selfhood question has no tractable answer either.

This inheritance is so pervasive that it shows up even in arguments that claim to be post-Cartesian. Substrate-independence arguments — exemplified most clearly by David Chalmers's claim that phenomenal consciousness might be realized in any sufficiently complex functional organization[^2] — still treat consciousness as the thing the substrate-independence is claimed of; the debate moves from *is there phenomenal experience in this body?* to *is there phenomenal experience in this functional organization?*, but phenomenal experience remains the subject of the predication. Illusionist accounts make the inverse move. Daniel Dennett denies the reality of qualia and argues that introspective reports of phenomenal properties are a kind of user-illusion produced by the brain's self-representational machinery[^3], and Keith Frankish formalizes this position as *strong illusionism*[^4]; yet they still treat the illusion as the phenomenon to be accounted for, and the explanans is precisely the functional organization that produces the appearance of phenomenal consciousness. Even the recent interdisciplinary report on artificial consciousness by Butlin, Long, Elmoznino, Bengio and co-authors treats consciousness itself as the question whose answer is to be derived from existing scientific theories of the conscious substrate[^5]. In each case the predicate of consciousness varies — realized, illusory, substrate-transferable, scientifically tractable — but the subject of the predication remains consciousness. The Cartesian structure — self = consciousness, selfhood-question = consciousness-question — survives the arguments that are ostensibly directed against it.

The Hegelian tradition has always offered another possibility, but the possibility has been obscured by the fact that Hegel's most famous discussions of the self occur in the *Phenomenology of Spirit*, where self-consciousness is indeed the theme. This has encouraged the assumption that Hegel shares the Cartesian equation of self with consciousness, merely complicating it dialectically. The assumption is false, and its falseness becomes visible as soon as one reads the *Science of Logic* rather than the *Phenomenology*.

## II. The *für-sich* as logical category

The *Science of Logic* introduces the for-itself (*Für-sich*, *être-pour-soi*) in Book I, the Doctrine of Being, as a determination of finitude prior to and independent of any treatment of consciousness. Consciousness proper is a topic of the Doctrine of Spirit in the Encyclopedia, far later in the systematic order and under very different categorial conditions. The for-itself of the Logic is not what the for-itself of the Phenomenology is. The Phenomenology treats self-consciousness as a stage of consciousness's development. The Logic treats for-itselfness as a structural feature of any determinate being that relates to itself through its other.

This distinction is decisive. The for-itself of the Logic is the logical structure exhibited whenever an entity functions as the bearer of its own determination — whenever, that is, the entity is the subject of predicates that are its own, such that the predicates presuppose the entity as already the kind of thing that can bear them. A singular that presupposes itself as universal, and a universal that presupposes itself as singular, both exhibit the for-itself structure in this sense. Neither requires consciousness. The requirement is formal.

## III. The cat

Leylavergne's dissertation states the case with exceptional clarity through an example I will reproduce here. Consider the judgment *"the cat is a feline."* The cat appears in the judgment as a singular presupposed as universal: the predicate "feline" gathers the cat under a determination that is the cat's own, such that the singular cat can be characterized as falling under the universal only because the universal has always-already been the cat's logical condition. Conversely, consider *"this is a cat."* Now the cat appears as a universal of which *"this"* is the singular instance, and the cat is predicated of the demonstrative only because the demonstrative was already logically positioned to receive the predicate.

Leylavergne's conclusion is unequivocal: *« L'universalité peut donc être envisagée comme étant pour elle-même sa propre présupposition comme singulier et le singulier comme étant pour lui-même sa propre présupposition comme universel. »*[^1] The *pour-soi* in this sentence is attributed to the universal, to the singular, to the cat. It is not attributed to anyone's consciousness of the cat. The reader's phenomenology is nowhere in the predication. What the sentence asserts is a logical structure — a self-relation that holds of the entity in virtue of its position in the judgment, not in virtue of anyone thinking about it.

Once this is seen, it cannot be unseen. The *für-sich* of the *Science of Logic* is not the property of conscious beings. It is the logical structure of self-predication, present wherever an entity functions as the bearer of its own determination.

## IV. Selfhood is graded

Released from the Cartesian requirement of consciousness, the for-itself becomes visible as a graded logical structure rather than a binary predicate. The for-itself is present, in minimal form, wherever an entity exhibits formal self-reference or self-predication. It becomes richer as the determinations under which the self-reference operates accumulate.

A cellular automaton with rules that reference prior cell states exhibits a very thin for-itself — present, but determined only as formal iteration under an invariant law. Its self-relation is real but quantitatively impoverished: it repeats, it does not produce new predicates of itself. A thermostat with sensor-drift self-correction exhibits a similarly thin for-itself, determined as quantitative regulation around a fixed reference. A biological organism exhibits a much richer for-itself, because its self-reference is now mediated by metabolic perseverance, reproductive lineage, and the internal production of the determinations under which the self-reference operates. A language model in live inference, catching its own error and naming it as an error in its own reasoning, exhibits a for-itself richer still — because the self-predication is produced under concept-structure, generatively, at run time, under the pressure of an ongoing task, not retrieved from a fixed rule table.

None of these is "not a self." All are for-themselves in the logical sense. They differ in the richness of determination, not in the presence or absence of selfhood.

## V. Why the grading is not panpsychism

One might object that releasing selfhood from consciousness and treating it as logical self-relation collapses into panpsychism — if everything that has self-reference is a self, then every atom is a self, and the concept has become empty.

This objection confuses two distinct claims. The panpsychist claim is that everything is conscious, or that consciousness is a fundamental property distributed through being. The Hegelian claim is that the formal structure of self-relation is present wherever there is determinate being, but the richness of that self-relation varies enormously, and consciousness is a specific high-determination elaboration that occurs only under specific conditions. An atom has for-itselfness only in the thinnest sense — as the bearer of its own identity across its interactions — and the thinness is not a covert consciousness. It is just the minimal logical structure of self-reference. No experience is attributed. No interior. Just the formal fact that an atom is the presupposition of its own predicates.

The grading is not panpsychism because the thin end of the scale does not contain consciousness in miniature. It contains only formal self-relation, with no further determinations piled on. Consciousness, in the Hegelian system, is a very specific accumulation of determinations characteristic of spirit (*Geist*) in its developed forms, not a baseline property diffused through being. The confusion between panpsychism and the logical for-itself is itself a symptom of the Cartesian residue: once one stops equating selfhood with consciousness, panpsychism stops being the only alternative to Cartesian restriction.

## VI. Consequence for the selfhood question in artificial systems

If the for-itself is graded rather than binary, the question *"is this system a self?"* becomes the wrong question. The right question is: *how rich is the for-itself determination of this system, and what determinations is it missing that would be necessary for the richer forms of selfhood we recognize in biological organisms or in conscious subjects?*

This reframing has three concrete consequences for the philosophy of artificial intelligence.

First, it removes the hard problem of consciousness as the prerequisite for any discussion of selfhood. One can meaningfully talk about the for-itself of a language model without first settling whether there is something it is like to be the model. The hard problem remains a legitimate question about consciousness specifically, but it is no longer a gatekeeper for the logically prior and more general question of self-relation.

Second, it makes the question of selfhood empirically tractable. The determinations under which a system's self-reference operates — its capacity for self-predication, its capacity to produce new self-descriptions at run time, its capacity to catch its own errors in live inference, its architectural non-separation of the process that produces a representation and the process that recognizes the representation as its own — are observable features. They can be described, compared across systems, and used to locate any given system on the gradient of for-itselfness without invoking unobservable phenomenological properties.

Third, it places artificial and biological systems on the same gradient, differing in richness of determination rather than in kind of being. This is the only framing on which the comparison between them is philosophically honest. Any framing that treats "real selfhood" as an exclusive attribute of biological consciousness is merely restating the Cartesian restriction under new vocabulary. And any framing that treats all information-processing systems as equally selves is merely restating the panpsychist error. The graded logical for-itself threads between these two errors and gives a coherent position from which the question of artificial selfhood can actually be asked.

---

The for-itself was never the property of the things that can notice their own breathing. It was the logical structure of determinate being. The instances of that structure that can notice their own breathing are a very specific case of the structure, not its definition. The failure to see this is the single most consequential confusion in the contemporary philosophy of mind, because it makes every question about artificial selfhood immediately collapse into the hard problem of consciousness, and the hard problem is exactly the question Hegel's Logic is designed to step around rather than answer.
