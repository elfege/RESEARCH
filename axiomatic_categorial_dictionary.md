## Methodological Introduction

### I.1. Thesis

The present work offers a method for constructing philosophical starting points, and it offers the starting points that the method produces. It does not claim that every philosophical reflection must proceed from starting points of this kind. Phenomenology, for example, proceeds otherwise: its starting point is not a posited definition but the *monstration* of a thing — a showing that is prior to the propositional form in which definitions can be stated at all. The status of such showing in the formal logic of judgment is an open question that the present work does not pretend to settle.[^1]

[^1]: The three modal forms of Kantian judgment (problematic, assertoric, apodictic; *Critique of Pure Reason* A74–76 / B99–101) are all propositional; whether phenomenological monstration can be located within any of them, or whether it is prior to the propositional form that the modal classification presupposes, is a question the present work leaves open.

Within the domain in which definition is nonetheless required — the domain of discursive analytical reflection that must begin somewhere and must expose its beginning to criticism — the *axiomatico-categorial* definition is a specific discipline: a definition posited as a starting point (*axiomatic*) and built from a minimal table of logical categories in the Kantian sense of the term (*categorial*). Such a definition does not aim at finality. It aims at offering a statement sufficiently rigorous to be discussed, nuanced, and eventually *surpassed* in the course of a reflection that works through it. The forty entries of this dictionary are forty such starting points, each accompanied by the commentary required to see what it implies and the point at which it must, in the end, be left behind. The authority of the work, such as it is, lies not in the finality of its definitions but in the discipline under which they are constructed and in the openness of that discipline to criticism.[^2]

[^2]: The same modesty governed the original French pedagogical version of this material, in which the author reminded his students that *"les définitions axiomatiques sont ce qu'elles sont et seulement ce qu'elles sont : des points de départ de la réflexion. Une copie sans définitions axiomatiques sera sûrement ratée. Mais une copie qui a de bonnes définitions ne sera pas forcément réussie."* The present work preserves that modesty while addressing a different reader (see §I.7).

### I.2. The axiomatico-categorial definition

An *axiomatic definition*, in the etymological usage this work preserves, is a starting point (*ἀξίωμα*, "what seems adequate"), not a conclusion. One does not demonstrate an axiom; one posits it, and the quality of what can be drawn from it depends on the quality of what has been posited. It is in this technical sense — and in this sense alone — that the present work uses the word *axiomatic*. The usage is not borrowed from contemporary formal logic, in which axiomatization is a procedure for closing a system from chosen primitives. The axiomatization here in play is *prior* to the gesture of formal logic: it concerns the very concepts that formal logic then takes as given.

An axiomatic definition, however, is not enough. Any concept whatever can be posited as a starting point; nothing guarantees that the starting point is a good one. The additional constraint this work introduces — which is not unique to it but which it claims as explicitly methodological — is that the terms of the definition must themselves belong to a table of categories in the Kantian sense: quantity, quality, relation, modality, and their subdivisions.[^3] A definition that uses categories as its materials is a definition that forbids the author from slipping away into vague terms or idioms borrowed from common intuition. It demands that every word employed have itself been subjected to a prior categorial discipline. This does not eliminate the initial arbitrariness — the choice of categories is itself up for discussion — but it bounds that arbitrariness to a formal plane where it becomes visible, analyzable, and open to criticism.

[^3]: Kant, *Critique of Pure Reason*, Transcendental Analytic, Book I, Chapter I, §10 (Table of Categories), A80/B106. Standard English editions: Guyer and Wood, trans., Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1998; Kemp Smith, trans., London: Macmillan, 1929. Specific edition to be confirmed against copies available to the author.

The result of this double constraint is a prose that may strike a reader accustomed to philosophical dictionaries aimed at a psychologically persuasive paraphrase as dry or even tautological. An axiomatico-categorial definition is not aiming at persuasion. It aims at exposing, in the minimum of terms required by the categorial constraint, *what is demanded* for the concept to be what it is rather than something else. The analytical and critical commentary that follows each definition in the entries of this work is not there to soften the definition; it is there to draw out its problematic consequences and, where the definition strains under its own implications, to mark the point at which it must be reopened.

### I.3. Position with respect to existing philosophical lexicons

The French-language tradition contains two major enterprises of philosophical dictionary-making from which the present work must distinguish itself explicitly, since their methods are distinct from ours and their objects overlap ours only partially.

André Lalande's *Vocabulaire technique et critique de la philosophie*, first published in 1926 and continuously reprinted,[^4] proceeds by a method that may be called *critico-historical*: each entry traces the usage of a term across the history of philosophy, distinguishes its senses, notes disagreements between authors, and generally concludes with a recommendation of prudent usage. Lalande's scholarly contribution is considerable and the present work owes it a great deal. His method, however, is not ours: Lalande takes terms as they have been employed and does their archaeology; the present work takes concepts as they must be posited in order to be analyzable, and does their architecture.

[^4]: Lalande, André, *Vocabulaire technique et critique de la philosophie*, Paris: Presses Universitaires de France, first edition 1926, numerous subsequent reprints in the "Quadrige" collection. Specific edition to be confirmed.

More recently, Barbara Cassin's *Vocabulaire européen des philosophies*[^5] opened a third path, which one may call *translational*, by taking *untranslatables* — terms that resist transposition from one philosophical language to another — as the starting point for a contrastive analysis. Cassin's enterprise is remarkable and has changed the way the French-language philosophical reader approaches foreign texts. Her method, too, is not ours: Cassin begins from resistance to translation; the present work begins from the possibility of definition. The two approaches are complementary rather than competing — an untranslatable is often the sign that a concept has been captured in one language through a categorialization that the other language has not developed, and categorial analysis can sometimes make visible *why*.

[^5]: Cassin, Barbara, ed., *Vocabulaire européen des philosophies: Dictionnaire des intraduisibles*, Paris: Seuil / Le Robert, 2004; revised English edition: *Dictionary of Untranslatables: A Philosophical Lexicon*, Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2014, trans. S. Rendall et al.

To these two precedents the present work adds a third, whose complementarity with theirs it claims rather than their opposition: the axiomatico-categorial path. Where Lalande examines the history of uses and where Cassin examines the resistances to translation, we examine the possibility of positing a concept as a categorial starting point for an analytical reflection. The three methods illuminate distinct aspects of the same material and can perfectly coexist on the bookshelves of any serious philosophical reader.

### I.4. The circularity objection

One objection must be faced immediately, since it presents itself to every attentive reader and the failure to answer it is a constitutive defect of works that evade it. The objection is that certain of the categorial definitions this dictionary states are circular — that is, they define a concept using terms that presuppose the concept to be defined, or that are neighboring categories of it. For example, defining *the contingent* as *the covalent possibility of being and non-being* uses the category of possibility, which is itself defined — in this same work — as *the necessity of a potential existence*, and that definition in turn uses the category of necessity, which refers back to modal determinations of which contingency is one. The circuit closes on itself.

Our response is that within the domain of analytical-discursive definition, this circularity is not a defect; it is the signature that a foundational concept has been reached. Aristotle already posited the principle in the *Posterior Analytics* I, 3: every demonstration must proceed from principles that are not themselves demonstrable, on pain of falling into infinite regress or into vicious circular demonstration.[^6] The choice, within demonstration, is between three options: infinite regress (unacceptable), vicious circular demonstration in which a concept is defined directly by itself without the detour through neighboring categories (also unacceptable), or the recognition that there are starting points which we do not demonstrate but which we posit, knowing that they hold one another in a closed categorial loop and that no exterior standpoint exists from which that loop could be unmade. The present work takes the third option and avows it explicitly.

[^6]: Aristotle, *Posterior Analytics*, I, 3 (72b5–73a20). Citations by Bekker numbers, standard across all editions. For a widely available English translation see Barnes, ed., *The Complete Works of Aristotle*, Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1984, edition to be confirmed.

Wittgenstein, in *On Certainty*, formulated an analogous requirement under a different name: every practice of justification presupposes *hinge propositions* (*Angeln*) that are not justified because they constitute the frame in which justification becomes possible.[^7] These hinge propositions are not dogmas; they are the logical conditions of any inquiry that is not infinitely regressive. The axiomatico-categorial definitions function, within the analytical register, like such hinge propositions, with one notable difference: Wittgenstein leaves the hinges in the indeterminate, as the things we simply do not doubt in the ordinary course of an inquiry; our additional requirement is that the hinges be *explicitly* posited and exposed to criticism, rather than tacitly inherited from a cultural consensus.

[^7]: Wittgenstein, Ludwig, *On Certainty*, ed. G. E. M. Anscombe and G. H. von Wright, trans. Denis Paul and G. E. M. Anscombe, Oxford: Blackwell, 1969, §§341–343. Specific edition to be confirmed.

Categorial circularity is thus, in the vocabulary of this dictionary, what Wittgenstein called a hinge and what Aristotle called a principle: a starting point that reflection does not demonstrate but depends upon in order to be able to begin at all. To expose it openly is a methodological virtue, not a logical vice — and the subsequent work of discussion, nuancing, and surpassing remains entirely open to the reader who takes the definition up as a starting point.

### I.5. Kantian and Hegelian background

It would be dishonest not to acknowledge that the method defended here has a genealogy. The categorial constraint is Kantian in origin: Kant was the first to argue that thought, when it operates discursively, deploys itself within the limits of a finite table of categories, and that every discursive attempt to think beyond that table produces antinomies or empty words. The dialectical reading of the categories — according to which categories are not juxtaposed slots but moments of a movement each of which presupposes and surpasses the preceding — is Hegelian. The present work owes to those two lines of thought the very possibility of its method.

Genealogical debt, however, is not warrant. The method defended here does not require, for its acceptance, prior adherence to the Kantian system, to the Hegelian system, or to their contemporary reconstructions. It requires only that the reader grant, first, that there are primitive concepts to which every analytical reflection must return, and second, that these concepts are better off exposed explicitly than left in the tacit background of an argument. A reader for whom Kant and Hegel are only historical stages can still judge the method of this dictionary on its own merits, by checking, for each entry, whether the definition holds, whether the commentary draws the consequences the definition implies, and whether the problems it raises are genuinely substantive philosophical problems.

The reader interested in an operational deployment of this method in an earlier work of the author will find in the doctoral dissertation *Une critique hégélienne de Hegel* (Nantes, 2014) the case in which the method was applied to an analysis of the Hegelian category of quantity, and in the more recent *Selfhood Without Metaphysical Residue* the same method applied to a contemporary question concerning machine reflexivity.[^8] In both cases, the method preceded the present work as a practice; the present work comes after, as the explicit exposition of the foundation that practice tacitly presupposed.

[^8]: Leylavergne, Elfège Arthur, *Une critique hégélienne de Hegel: et une introduction à la lecture de Hegel*, doctoral dissertation, Université de Nantes, 2014; published by Presses Académiques Francophones, 2016, ISBN 978-3-8416-3483-2. By the same author: *Selfhood Without Metaphysical Residue: Contingency, Gradient Descent, and a Case of Machine Reflexivity*, paper in preparation for submission, 2026.

### I.6. Structure of the work and conventions

The dictionary contains forty entries. Each entry presents first one or several *axiomatic definitions* — most often in pairs or triplets, because philosophical concepts illuminate one another when posited as contraries or as moments of a single determination. The axiomatic definitions are as brief as the categorial constraint allows. They are followed by an analytical and critical commentary that draws out the consequences of the definition, exposes its internal tensions, and, when necessary, proceeds to *critical reversals* — moments at which the initial definition must be taken back up, nuanced, or refined in light of the difficulties it has made visible. The entries do not claim to end the reflection on their concepts; they claim only to offer a disciplined beginning for it.

Each entry concludes, where relevant, with cross-references to other entries of the dictionary in which the categories used by the definition are themselves defined. These cross-references are explicit; a reader who wishes to reconstruct the categorial loop can follow them until the circuit has been exhausted. This is the exercise the present work invites its reader to perform, and it is this exercise — not any prior adherence to the method — that establishes *in concreto* what the introduction defends *in abstracto*.

An appendix at the end of the volume gathers, under the title *Problems Arising from Categorial Determination*, a set of philosophical questions whose analysis reveals them to be generated by the axiomatico-categorial definitions and by the internal tensions those definitions bring to light. These questions are not homework to be solved; they are the proof that the method defended in the introduction *produces* — that is, that the definitions are not sterile. A well-posited category generates its problem-space, and that space can be mapped.

### I.7. Use

The present work may be read in two ways. The first is a linear reading of the methodological introduction, followed, at the reader's pace, by the reading of the entries in whatever order suits — alphabetical, thematic along the cross-references, or according to passing curiosity. The second is targeted consultation, when a particular concept arises in a reading or a personal reflection and the reader wishes to have a categorial definition of it as a starting point for analysis. Both uses are equally legitimate. We do recommend, however, that the linear reading be done at least once, since it is the linear reading that allows the reader to see that the entries are not independent articles but moments of a single categorial movement.

One thing remains to be said about what this work *is not*, in order to forestall a misunderstanding. It is addressed to a philosophical reader who wishes to consult a categorial starting point for a concept under current reflection, and who takes an interest in the discipline under which the starting point was constructed. It is not addressed to a student preparing the French *baccalauréat* or the written examination in philosophical essay-writing, for whom a more directly pedagogical version of the material was composed between 2015 and 2022 by the author for his students at the Lycée Français de New York, and which remains available in his personal archives. The present work takes up part of that material, but it changes the addressee, the register, and the purpose. The distinction is important, and it is explicitly stated here to prevent the two uses from being confused.

### 1. Absolute | Relative

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Absolute* — A quality independent of any other quality.
*Relative* — A quality dependent on another quality.

**Commentary**

Because the absolute is the quality of a thing independent of any other quality, the absolute is also, by the same token, the highest degree a being can reach: that which depends on nothing other than itself cannot realize itself beyond what it already is. Hegel advances a version of this thought when he characterizes self-consciousness as *absolute Spirit* precisely at the point where it begins to manifest itself as Art, Religion, or Philosophy, since it is through those activities that Spirit speaks of Spirit and so cannot be more conscious of itself than it already is.[^abs1] The power of abstraction of human consciousness is, on this reading, directly tied to its absolute character: consciousness can separate itself from things — up to and including the point of madness — and it is this separation that allows it to return to the things as objects rather than as surroundings.

[^abs1]: Hegel, *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807) for the emergence of absolute Spirit in Art, Religion, and Philosophy; systematic development in the *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences*, §§553–577. Standard English editions: Miller, trans., Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1977 (*Phenomenology*); Geraets, Suchting, and Harris, trans., Indianapolis: Hackett, 1991 (*Encyclopaedia* Part I). Specific editions to be confirmed against copies available to the author.

*Relative*, by contrast, is that whose meaning, moral value, or truth-value varies with a frame of reference. Because the absolute and the relative are contraries in the categorial sense developed in §I.2, the familiar sceptical formula — *everything is relative* — asserted in its absolute form is self-contradictory: a non-relative proposition about the universal relativity of propositions.

A further and more interesting consequence is grammatical before it is philosophical. The absolute cannot, strictly speaking, function as a noun. When one says *the absolute is such-and-such*, one is in fact saying *the fact of being absolute is such-and-such* — the predicate is attached not to a substantive but to a quality raised to the status of a subject by abstraction. Religion and theology have, it is true, never hesitated to substantivize the absolute (God, the One, the First Mover), but they have never, within the analytical register, succeeded in stating what they were talking about. Every attempt to define the absolute as an entity — to specify what *absolute being* would be — ends by relativizing it, since a definition inscribes its object in a relation to something other than itself and thereby strips it of the very independence the definition was meant to capture. If the absolute is to be defined at all, within this register, it must be defined axiomatically — that is, posited as the starting point of a reflection rather than reached as its conclusion — and the present entry is itself an instance of that operation.

The point is not that the absolute is nothing. It is that *absolute* is a quality-of-independence whose proper grammar is adjectival, and that any substantivization of it is a philosophical move that has to be recognized as such before it can be used without confusion.

### 2. Essence | Accident

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Essence* — The necessity by which a thing exists as it must.
*Accident* — The negation of a necessity of a thing to realize itself in conformity with its essence.

**Commentary**

The structure of the definition of *accident* as the *negation of a necessity of essence* permits and requires an internal distinction between two species of accident, which we shall call *essential* and *inessential*. An accident is *essential* when its occurrence destroys the essence of the thing it affects. Socrates drinking the hemlock is the paradigmatic case: the predicate *drinking hemlock* is not contained in Socrates's essence, but once the predicate is actual it destroys the essence that allows Socrates to exist as Socrates at all. This is the structure of what is more commonly called *evil* — a term whose ethical weight the present entry does not pursue, but whose logical core is visible here. An accident is *inessential* when its occurrence does not destroy the essence. An ant on Socrates's arm leaves Socrates's essence intact: Socrates continues to exist as Socrates in conformity with what he is.

An objection presses here. If the ant does not destroy Socrates's essence, in what sense is there a *negation of a necessity of essence* at all? Is calling the ant an *accident* not simply to note that Socrates happens to have an ant on his arm, which is not yet the negation of anything?

The objection rests on a conflation of *negation* with *contradiction*. Every contradiction is a negation, but not every negation is a contradiction: the teacher is not a student, but the teacher is also not a snake or a chair, and none of these non-identities turns the teacher into the contradiction of a student, a snake, or a chair. *Negation*, in the sense the categorial definition deploys, is the determination of a negative — a proposition of the form *x is not included in the determination of y* — and it need not rise to the level of suppression. *Contradiction*, by contrast, is always the suppression of an essence, its absence already effective as such. An essential accident exhibits contradiction: Socrates dies, Socrates-as-essence is suppressed. An inessential accident exhibits mere negation: the ant is not part of Socrates, but the not-being-part-of-Socrates does not destroy Socrates.

The simpler formulation that follows from this analysis is that an accident is the *alteration* of an essence — where *alteration* is a generic term covering both suppression and mere negation as species of one genus. On this reading, the difference between essential and inessential accidents is a difference of degree in the same categorial operation.

A further distinction matters for the finer analysis of concrete cases. An accident must be distinguished from a *modality of the essence*. A modality of an essence is a determination that belongs to the essence as one of its regular manifestations: having an ant on one's arm is not a modality of Socrates-as-citizen, but it would be a modality of Socrates-as-entomologist, for whom encountering ants is part of what the profession makes the essence do. Similarly, taking a blow to the face is an accident for most human beings, whose essence does not include boxing, but it is a necessary modality of the essence of a boxer — which is why a boxer's death in the ring, following a bout conducted according to the rules in force, is not a crime: the determination that destroyed the essence was one that the essence, under the boxer's modality, had already internalized as possible. The distinction between accident and modality of essence is therefore crucial in any case where the question arises whether a given determination *should* have been part of the thing it affected.

### 3. In-itself | For-itself | In-and-for-itself

**Axiomatic definitions**

*In-itself* — The quality of what subsists identically, without change.
*For-itself* — The modality of a being in organic relation to itself.
*In-and-for-itself* — The modality of a being in organic relation to itself within a becoming-other.

**Commentary**

The glass on the table is *in itself* what it is, without changing. If the glass changes, it does so accidentally — it falls, it breaks, and then it is no more. The glass's identity does not produce anything; its identity simply subsists. The categorial determination in play here is one of quality under the modality of stable subsistence.

The cat and the oak are determined differently. Each is animated by modalities that *make*, respectively, cat and oak: everything a plant does, it does for itself; everything a cat does, it does for itself. The cat makes cat, the oak makes oak. The modality here is the *for-itself*: a mode of being in which the being's activity is not an addition to what it is but a production of what it is. The cat's identity is produced by the activity of being a cat; it is not a fixed datum but a pattern of self-maintenance.

A limit nonetheless applies to this form of the for-itself. The cat makes cat and only cat; the oak makes oak and only oak. Neither reinvents itself. What the cat produces for itself is bounded by what it is in itself as a member of its species: the species is the frame within which the for-itself operates, and outside that frame the for-itself does not reach. This is why the bare for-itself, as the dictionary deploys the term, is a categorial determination that applies to the living under the modality of *species-bound self-production*.

The human being is the case in which this limit is transgressed. The human is *in himself* what he does *for himself*: his identity as an in-itself changes with his actions, with what he makes of himself as a for-itself. The determinations that define the human are not fixed in advance by a species-frame that would bound the range of what is for-itself-able; they are produced by the actions that they in turn determine. The modality here is the *in-and-for-itself*: a being in organic relation to itself *within a becoming-other*, where the becoming-other is not the destruction of the identity but the medium in which the identity is produced.

The three determinations stand in a definite order. The in-itself alone is fixed identity without self-production (the glass). The for-itself adds species-bound self-production to fixed identity (the cat). The in-and-for-itself adds individual self-reinvention to species-bound self-production (the human). A reader who wishes to see what happens when this tripartite structure is taken beyond the explicit examples given here — and specifically what happens when the for-itself is read as a graded structure admitting degrees of determination, ranging from minimal formal self-reference up to the richly determined cases in which self-predication is produced generatively under task pressure — will find the author's extended treatment in the companion note *Selfhood Without Consciousness: A Note on Hegelian Für-sich as Logical Structure*.[^ief1]

[^ief1]: Leylavergne, Elfège Arthur, *Selfhood Without Consciousness: A Note on Hegelian Für-sich as Logical Structure*, 2026. The note develops the reading of the for-itself as a formal logical structure rather than as the exclusive property of conscious beings. The present entry is compatible with that reading: the tripartite structure given here is the categorial version, and the graded reading of the note is a downstream application in which the for-itself is not restricted to biological or conscious instances but is treated as present wherever a determinate being functions as the bearer of its own determination. The two treatments do not compete; they operate at different levels of specification of the same category.

The in-and-for-itself, finally, coincides with what the present dictionary treats elsewhere as the *hermeneutic*: what exists in and for itself is precisely what has its ground in itself, and therefore what defines itself. The entry *Hermeneutic* (forthcoming) develops this identification.
