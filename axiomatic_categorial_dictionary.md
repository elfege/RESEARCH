## Methodological Introduction

### I.1. Thesis

The present work offers a method for constructing philosophical starting points, and it offers the starting points that the method produces. It does not claim that every philosophical reflection must proceed from starting points of this kind. Phenomenology, for example, proceeds otherwise: its starting point is not a posited definition but the *monstration* of a thing — a showing that is prior to the propositional form in which definitions can be stated at all. The status of such showing in the formal logic of judgment is an open question that the present work does not pretend to settle.[^1]

[^1]: The three modal forms of Kantian judgment (problematic, assertoric, apodictic; *Critique of Pure Reason* A74–76 / B99–101) are all propositional; whether phenomenological monstration can be located within any of them, or whether it is prior to the propositional form that the modal classification presupposes, is a question the present work leaves open.

Within the domain in which definition is nonetheless required — the domain of discursive analytical reflection that must begin somewhere and must expose its beginning to criticism — the *axiomatico-categorial* definition is a specific discipline: a definition posited as a starting point (*axiomatic*) and built from a minimal table of logical categories in the Kantian sense of the term (*categorial*). Such a definition does not aim at finality. It aims at offering a statement sufficiently rigorous to be discussed, nuanced, and eventually *surpassed* in the course of a reflection that works through it. The thirty-nine entries of this dictionary are thirty-nine such starting points, each accompanied by the commentary required to see what it implies and the point at which it must, in the end, be left behind. The authority of the work, such as it is, lies not in the finality of its definitions but in the discipline under which they are constructed and in the openness of that discipline to criticism.[^2]

[^2]: The same modesty governed the original French pedagogical version of this material, in which the author reminded his students that *"les définitions axiomatiques sont ce qu'elles sont et seulement ce qu'elles sont : des points de départ de la réflexion. Une copie sans définitions axiomatiques sera sûrement ratée. Mais une copie qui a de bonnes définitions ne sera pas forcément réussie."* The present work preserves that modesty while addressing a different reader (see §I.7).

### I.2. The axiomatico-categorial definition

An *axiomatic definition*, in the etymological usage this work preserves, is a starting point (*ἀξίωμα*, "what seems adequate"), not a conclusion. One does not demonstrate an axiom; one posits it, and the quality of what can be drawn from it depends on the quality of what has been posited. It is in this technical sense — and in this sense alone — that the present work uses the word *axiomatic*. The usage is not borrowed from contemporary formal logic, in which axiomatization is a procedure for closing a system from chosen primitives. The axiomatization here in play is *prior* to the gesture of formal logic: it concerns the very concepts that formal logic then takes as given.

An axiomatic definition, however, is not enough. Any concept whatever can be posited as a starting point; nothing guarantees that the starting point is a good one. The additional constraint this work introduces — which is not unique to it but which it claims as explicitly methodological — is that the terms of the definition must themselves belong to a table of categories in the Kantian sense: quantity, quality, relation, modality, and their subdivisions.[^3] A definition that uses categories as its materials is a definition that forbids the author from slipping away into vague terms or idioms borrowed from common intuition. It demands that every word employed have itself been subjected to a prior categorial discipline. This does not eliminate the initial arbitrariness — the choice of categories is itself up for discussion — but it bounds that arbitrariness to a formal plane where it becomes visible, analyzable, and open to criticism.

[^3]: Kant, *Critique of Pure Reason*, Transcendental Analytic, Book I, Chapter I, §10 (Table of Categories), A80/B106. Standard English editions: Guyer and Wood, trans., Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1998; Kemp Smith, trans., London: Macmillan, 1929. Specific edition to be confirmed against copies available to the author.

The result of this double constraint is a prose that may strike a reader accustomed to philosophical dictionaries aimed at a psychologically persuasive paraphrase as dry or even tautological. An axiomatico-categorial definition is not aiming at persuasion. It aims at exposing, in the minimum of terms required by the categorial constraint, *what is demanded* for the concept to be what it is rather than something else. The analytical and critical commentary that follows each definition in the entries of this work is not there to soften the definition; it is there to draw out its problematic consequences and, where the definition strains under its own implications, to mark the point at which it must be reopened.

### I.3. Position with respect to existing philosophical lexicons

The French-language tradition contains two major enterprises of philosophical dictionary-making from which the present work must distinguish itself explicitly, since their methods are distinct from ours and their objects overlap ours only partially.

André Lalande's *Vocabulaire technique et critique de la philosophie*, first published in 1926 and continuously reprinted,[^4] proceeds by a method that may be called *critico-historical*: each entry traces the usage of a term across the history of philosophy, distinguishes its senses, notes disagreements between authors, and generally concludes with a recommendation of prudent usage. Lalande's scholarly contribution is considerable and the present work owes it a great deal. His method, however, is not ours: Lalande takes terms as they have been employed and does their archaeology; the present work takes concepts as they must be posited in order to be analyzable, and does their architecture.

[^4]: Lalande, André, *Vocabulaire technique et critique de la philosophie*, Paris: Presses Universitaires de France, first edition 1926, numerous subsequent reprints in the "Quadrige" collection. Specific edition to be confirmed.

More recently, Barbara Cassin's *Vocabulaire européen des philosophies*[^5] opened a third path, which one may call *translational*, by taking *untranslatables* — terms that resist transposition from one philosophical language to another — as the starting point for a contrastive analysis. Cassin's enterprise is remarkable and has changed the way the French-language philosophical reader approaches foreign texts. Her method, too, is not ours: Cassin begins from resistance to translation; the present work begins from the possibility of definition. The two approaches are complementary rather than competing — an untranslatable is often the sign that a concept has been captured in one language through a categorialization that the other language has not developed, and categorial analysis can sometimes make visible *why*.

[^5]: Cassin, Barbara, ed., *Vocabulaire européen des philosophies: Dictionnaire des intraduisibles*, Paris: Seuil / Le Robert, 2004; revised English edition: *Dictionary of Untranslatables: A Philosophical Lexicon*, Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2014, trans. S. Rendall et al.

To these two precedents the present work adds a third, whose complementarity with theirs it claims rather than their opposition: the axiomatico-categorial path. Where Lalande examines the history of uses and where Cassin examines the resistances to translation, we examine the possibility of positing a concept as a categorial starting point for an analytical reflection. The three methods illuminate distinct aspects of the same material and can perfectly coexist on the bookshelves of any serious philosophical reader.

### I.4. The circularity objection

One objection must be faced immediately, since it presents itself to every attentive reader and the failure to answer it is a constitutive defect of works that evade it. The objection is that certain of the categorial definitions this dictionary states are circular — that is, they define a concept using terms that presuppose the concept to be defined, or that are neighboring categories of it. For example, defining *the contingent* as *the covalent possibility of being and non-being* uses the category of possibility, which is itself defined — in this same work — as *the necessity of a potential existence*, and that definition in turn uses the category of necessity, which refers back to modal determinations of which contingency is one. The circuit closes on itself.

Our response is that within the domain of analytical-discursive definition, this circularity is not a defect; it is the signature that a foundational concept has been reached. Aristotle already posited the principle in the *Posterior Analytics* I, 3: every demonstration must proceed from principles that are not themselves demonstrable, on pain of falling into infinite regress or into vicious circular demonstration.[^6] The choice, within demonstration, is between three options: infinite regress (unacceptable), vicious circular demonstration in which a concept is defined directly by itself without the detour through neighboring categories (also unacceptable), or the recognition that there are starting points which we do not demonstrate but which we posit, knowing that they hold one another in a closed categorial loop and that no exterior standpoint exists from which that loop could be unmade. The present work takes the third option and avows it explicitly.

[^6]: Aristotle, *Posterior Analytics*, I, 3 (72b5–73a20). Citations by Bekker numbers, standard across all editions. For a widely available English translation see Barnes, ed., *The Complete Works of Aristotle*, Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1984, edition to be confirmed.

Wittgenstein, in *On Certainty*, formulated an analogous requirement under a different name: every practice of justification presupposes *hinge propositions* (*Angeln*) that are not justified because they constitute the frame in which justification becomes possible.[^7] These hinge propositions are not dogmas; they are the logical conditions of any inquiry that is not infinitely regressive. The axiomatico-categorial definitions function, within the analytical register, like such hinge propositions, with one notable difference: Wittgenstein leaves the hinges in the indeterminate, as the things we simply do not doubt in the ordinary course of an inquiry; our additional requirement is that the hinges be *explicitly* posited and exposed to criticism, rather than tacitly inherited from a cultural consensus.

[^7]: Wittgenstein, Ludwig, *On Certainty*, ed. G. E. M. Anscombe and G. H. von Wright, trans. Denis Paul and G. E. M. Anscombe, Oxford: Blackwell, 1969, §§341–343. Specific edition to be confirmed.

Categorial circularity is thus, in the vocabulary of this dictionary, what Wittgenstein called a hinge and what Aristotle called a principle: a starting point that reflection does not demonstrate but depends upon in order to be able to begin at all. To expose it openly is a methodological virtue, not a logical vice — and the subsequent work of discussion, nuancing, and surpassing remains entirely open to the reader who takes the definition up as a starting point.

### I.5. Kantian and Hegelian background

It would be dishonest not to acknowledge that the method defended here has a genealogy. The categorial constraint is Kantian in origin: Kant was the first to argue that thought, when it operates discursively, deploys itself within the limits of a finite table of categories, and that every discursive attempt to think beyond that table produces antinomies or empty words. The dialectical reading of the categories — according to which categories are not juxtaposed slots but moments of a movement each of which presupposes and surpasses the preceding — is Hegelian. The present work owes to those two lines of thought the very possibility of its method.

Genealogical debt, however, is not warrant. The method defended here does not require, for its acceptance, prior adherence to the Kantian system, to the Hegelian system, or to their contemporary reconstructions. It requires only that the reader grant, first, that there are primitive concepts to which every analytical reflection must return, and second, that these concepts are better off exposed explicitly than left in the tacit background of an argument. A reader for whom Kant and Hegel are only historical stages can still judge the method of this dictionary on its own merits, by checking, for each entry, whether the definition holds, whether the commentary draws the consequences the definition implies, and whether the problems it raises are genuinely substantive philosophical problems.

The reader interested in an operational deployment of this method in an earlier work of the author will find in the doctoral dissertation *Une critique hégélienne de Hegel* (Nantes, 2014) the case in which the method was applied to an analysis of the Hegelian category of quantity, and in the more recent *Selfhood Without Metaphysical Residue* the same method applied to a contemporary question concerning machine reflexivity.[^8] In both cases, the method preceded the present work as a practice; the present work comes after, as the explicit exposition of the foundation that practice tacitly presupposed.

[^8]: Leylavergne, Elfège Arthur, *Une critique hégélienne de Hegel: et une introduction à la lecture de Hegel*, doctoral dissertation, Université de Nantes, 2014; published by Presses Académiques Francophones, 2016, ISBN 978-3-8416-3483-2. By the same author: *Selfhood Without Metaphysical Residue: Contingency, Gradient Descent, and a Case of Machine Reflexivity*, paper in preparation for submission, 2026.

### I.6. Structure of the work and conventions

The dictionary contains thirty-nine entries. Each entry presents first one or several *axiomatic definitions* — most often in pairs or triplets, because philosophical concepts illuminate one another when posited as contraries or as moments of a single determination. The axiomatic definitions are as brief as the categorial constraint allows. They are followed by an analytical and critical commentary that draws out the consequences of the definition, exposes its internal tensions, and, when necessary, proceeds to *critical reversals* — moments at which the initial definition must be taken back up, nuanced, or refined in light of the difficulties it has made visible. The entries do not claim to end the reflection on their concepts; they claim only to offer a disciplined beginning for it.

Each entry concludes, where relevant, with cross-references to other entries of the dictionary in which the categories used by the definition are themselves defined. These cross-references are explicit; a reader who wishes to reconstruct the categorial loop can follow them until the circuit has been exhausted. This is the exercise the present work invites its reader to perform, and it is this exercise — not any prior adherence to the method — that establishes *in concreto* what the introduction defends *in abstracto*.

An appendix at the end of the volume gathers, under the title *Problems Arising from Categorial Determination*, a set of philosophical questions whose analysis reveals them to be generated by the axiomatico-categorial definitions and by the internal tensions those definitions bring to light. These questions are not homework to be solved; they are the proof that the method defended in the introduction *produces* — that is, that the definitions are not sterile. A well-posited category generates its problem-space, and that space can be mapped.

### I.7. Use

The present work may be read in two ways. The first is a linear reading of the methodological introduction, followed, at the reader's pace, by the reading of the entries in whatever order suits — alphabetical, thematic along the cross-references, or according to passing curiosity. The second is targeted consultation, when a particular concept arises in a reading or a personal reflection and the reader wishes to have a categorial definition of it as a starting point for analysis. Both uses are equally legitimate. We do recommend, however, that the linear reading be done at least once, since it is the linear reading that allows the reader to see that the entries are not independent articles but moments of a single categorial movement.

One thing remains to be said about what this work *is not*, in order to forestall a misunderstanding. It is addressed to a philosophical reader who wishes to consult a categorial starting point for a concept under current reflection, and who takes an interest in the discipline under which the starting point was constructed. It is not addressed to a student preparing the French *baccalauréat* or the written examination in philosophical essay-writing, for whom a more directly pedagogical version of the material was composed between 2015 and 2022 by the author for his students at the Lycée Français de New York, and which remains available in his personal archives. The present work takes up part of that material, but it changes the addressee, the register, and the purpose. The distinction is important, and it is explicitly stated here to prevent the two uses from being confused.

### 1. Absolute | Relative

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Absolute* — A quality independent of any other quality.
*Relative* — A quality dependent on another quality.

**Commentary**

Because the absolute is the quality of a thing independent of any other quality, the absolute is also, by the same token, the highest degree a being can reach: that which depends on nothing other than itself cannot realize itself beyond what it already is. Hegel advances a version of this thought when he characterizes self-consciousness as *absolute Spirit* precisely at the point where it begins to manifest itself as Art, Religion, or Philosophy, since it is through those activities that Spirit speaks of Spirit and so cannot be more conscious of itself than it already is.[^abs1] The power of abstraction of human consciousness is, on this reading, directly tied to its absolute character: consciousness can separate itself from things — up to and including the point of madness — and it is this separation that allows it to return to the things as objects rather than as surroundings.

[^abs1]: Hegel, *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807) for the emergence of absolute Spirit in Art, Religion, and Philosophy; systematic development in the *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences*, §§553–577. Standard English editions: Miller, trans., Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1977 (*Phenomenology*); Geraets, Suchting, and Harris, trans., Indianapolis: Hackett, 1991 (*Encyclopaedia* Part I). Specific editions to be confirmed against copies available to the author.

*Relative*, by contrast, is that whose meaning, moral value, or truth-value varies with a frame of reference. Because the absolute and the relative are contraries in the categorial sense developed in §I.2, the familiar sceptical formula — *everything is relative* — asserted in its absolute form is self-contradictory: a non-relative proposition about the universal relativity of propositions.

A further and more interesting consequence is grammatical before it is philosophical. The absolute cannot, strictly speaking, function as a noun. When one says *the absolute is such-and-such*, one is in fact saying *the fact of being absolute is such-and-such* — the predicate is attached not to a substantive but to a quality raised to the status of a subject by abstraction. Religion and theology have, it is true, never hesitated to substantivize the absolute (God, the One, the First Mover), but they have never, within the analytical register, succeeded in stating what they were talking about. Every attempt to define the absolute as an entity — to specify what *absolute being* would be — ends by relativizing it, since a definition inscribes its object in a relation to something other than itself and thereby strips it of the very independence the definition was meant to capture. If the absolute is to be defined at all, within this register, it must be defined axiomatically — that is, posited as the starting point of a reflection rather than reached as its conclusion — and the present entry is itself an instance of that operation.

The point is not that the absolute is nothing. It is that *absolute* is a quality-of-independence whose proper grammar is adjectival, and that any substantivization of it is a philosophical move that has to be recognized as such before it can be used without confusion.

### 2. Essence | Accident

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Essence* — The necessity by which a thing exists as it must.
*Accident* — The negation of a necessity of a thing to realize itself in conformity with its essence.

**Commentary**

The structure of the definition of *accident* as the *negation of a necessity of essence* permits and requires an internal distinction between two species of accident, which we shall call *essential* and *inessential*. An accident is *essential* when its occurrence destroys the essence of the thing it affects. Socrates drinking the hemlock is the paradigmatic case: the predicate *drinking hemlock* is not contained in Socrates's essence, but once the predicate is actual it destroys the essence that allows Socrates to exist as Socrates at all. This is the structure of what is more commonly called *evil* — a term whose ethical weight the present entry does not pursue, but whose logical core is visible here. An accident is *inessential* when its occurrence does not destroy the essence. An ant on Socrates's arm leaves Socrates's essence intact: Socrates continues to exist as Socrates in conformity with what he is.

An objection presses here. If the ant does not destroy Socrates's essence, in what sense is there a *negation of a necessity of essence* at all? Is calling the ant an *accident* not simply to note that Socrates happens to have an ant on his arm, which is not yet the negation of anything?

The objection rests on a conflation of *negation* with *contradiction*. Every contradiction is a negation, but not every negation is a contradiction: the teacher is not a student, but the teacher is also not a snake or a chair, and none of these non-identities turns the teacher into the contradiction of a student, a snake, or a chair. *Negation*, in the sense the categorial definition deploys, is the determination of a negative — a proposition of the form *x is not included in the determination of y* — and it need not rise to the level of suppression. *Contradiction*, by contrast, is always the suppression of an essence, its absence already effective as such. An essential accident exhibits contradiction: Socrates dies, Socrates-as-essence is suppressed. An inessential accident exhibits mere negation: the ant is not part of Socrates, but the not-being-part-of-Socrates does not destroy Socrates.

The simpler formulation that follows from this analysis is that an accident is the *alteration* of an essence — where *alteration* is a generic term covering both suppression and mere negation as species of one genus. On this reading, the difference between essential and inessential accidents is a difference of degree in the same categorial operation.

A further distinction matters for the finer analysis of concrete cases. An accident must be distinguished from a *modality of the essence*. A modality of an essence is a determination that belongs to the essence as one of its regular manifestations: having an ant on one's arm is not a modality of Socrates-as-citizen, but it would be a modality of Socrates-as-entomologist, for whom encountering ants is part of what the profession makes the essence do. Similarly, taking a blow to the face is an accident for most human beings, whose essence does not include boxing, but it is a necessary modality of the essence of a boxer — which is why a boxer's death in the ring, following a bout conducted according to the rules in force, is not a crime: the determination that destroyed the essence was one that the essence, under the boxer's modality, had already internalized as possible. The distinction between accident and modality of essence is therefore crucial in any case where the question arises whether a given determination *should* have been part of the thing it affected.

On the relation between negation and contradiction in the broader context of the axiomatico-categorial method, see §I.2.

### 3. In-itself | For-itself | In-and-for-itself

**Axiomatic definitions**

*In-itself* — The quality of what subsists identically, without change.
*For-itself* — The modality of a being in organic relation to itself.
*In-and-for-itself* — The modality of a being in organic relation to itself within a becoming-other.

**Commentary**

The glass on the table is *in itself* what it is, without changing. If the glass changes, it does so accidentally — it falls, it breaks, and then it is no more. The glass's identity does not produce anything; its identity simply subsists. The categorial determination in play here is one of quality under the modality of stable subsistence.

The cat and the oak are determined differently. Each is animated by modalities that *make*, respectively, cat and oak: everything a plant does, it does for itself; everything a cat does, it does for itself. The cat makes cat, the oak makes oak. The modality here is the *for-itself*: a mode of being in which the being's activity is not an addition to what it is but a production of what it is. The cat's identity is produced by the activity of being a cat; it is not a fixed datum but a pattern of self-maintenance.

A limit nonetheless applies to this form of the for-itself. The cat makes cat and only cat; the oak makes oak and only oak. Neither reinvents itself. What the cat produces for itself is bounded by what it is in itself as a member of its species: the species is the frame within which the for-itself operates, and outside that frame the for-itself does not reach. This is why the bare for-itself, as the dictionary deploys the term, is a categorial determination that applies to the living under the modality of *species-bound self-production*.

The human being is the case in which this limit is transgressed. The human is *in himself* what he does *for himself*: his identity as an in-itself changes with his actions, with what he makes of himself as a for-itself. The determinations that define the human are not fixed in advance by a species-frame that would bound the range of what is for-itself-able; they are produced by the actions that they in turn determine. The modality here is the *in-and-for-itself*: a being in organic relation to itself *within a becoming-other*, where the becoming-other is not the destruction of the identity but the medium in which the identity is produced.

The three determinations stand in a definite order. The in-itself alone is fixed identity without self-production (the glass). The for-itself adds species-bound self-production to fixed identity (the cat). The in-and-for-itself adds individual self-reinvention to species-bound self-production (the human). A reader who wishes to see what happens when this tripartite structure is taken beyond the explicit examples given here — and specifically what happens when the for-itself is read as a graded structure admitting degrees of determination, ranging from minimal formal self-reference up to the richly determined cases in which self-predication is produced generatively under task pressure — will find the author's extended treatment in the companion note *Selfhood Without Consciousness: A Note on Hegelian Für-sich as Logical Structure*.[^ief1]

[^ief1]: Leylavergne, Elfège Arthur, *Selfhood Without Consciousness: A Note on Hegelian Für-sich as Logical Structure*, 2026. The note develops the reading of the for-itself as a formal logical structure rather than as the exclusive property of conscious beings. The present entry is compatible with that reading: the tripartite structure given here is the categorial version, and the graded reading of the note is a downstream application in which the for-itself is not restricted to biological or conscious instances but is treated as present wherever a determinate being functions as the bearer of its own determination. The two treatments do not compete; they operate at different levels of specification of the same category.

The in-and-for-itself, finally, coincides with what the present dictionary treats elsewhere as the *hermeneutic*: what exists in and for itself is precisely what has its ground in itself, and therefore what defines itself. The entry *Hermeneutic* (forthcoming) develops this identification.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* quality, modality, relation, identity, becoming. See the entries *Hermeneutic* (forthcoming), *Identity | Equality | Difference* (forthcoming), and the companion note *Selfhood Without Consciousness*.

### 4. Abstract | Concrete

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Abstract* — The logical quality of an idea as separated from reality.
*Concrete* — The ontological quality of a thing as reality.

**Commentary**

What is abstract is separated from reality. The circle is abstract; the Penrose triangle is abstract; the representation of one's duty in an impossible situation — the duty to resist a tyrant opposed to the duty not to kill — is abstract. Concrete reality, in such a case, will ensure that whatever one does, one acts contrary to one's duty, because duty is an abstract representation that does not fold back seamlessly onto the real.

The distinction operates not as a binary classification of things into two bins, but as a determination of the *register* in which a concept is being deployed. The same object — for instance, the State — can be described as an abstract representation (when considered as a juridical form, a set of norms, a constitutional architecture) or as a concrete reality (when considered as the set of actual power relations, bureaucratic apparatuses, armed forces, and material institutions that enforce those norms). Whether the State is abstract or concrete is not a question about the State; it is a question about the angle under which the analysis approaches it.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* quality, reality, separation. See the entries *Ideal | Real*, *Formal | Material*, and *Analysis | Synthesis*.

### 5. Act | Potency

**Axiomatic definitions**

*In act* — The quality of a thing that has fully realized all its possibilities.
*In potency* — The quality of an unrealized possibility.

**Commentary**

What is in act is realized, effective. What is in potency tends toward realization according to a certain end and a certain form but remains at the state of simple possibility. The acorn is an oak in potency; the child is a free adult in potency. The oak is an actual tree, fully realized — that is, in act.

The philosophical interest of the pair lies not in the static classification (realized vs. not-yet-realized) but in the zone where the two determinations overlap without coinciding. A student at the threshold of adulthood is neither an adult in act (not yet a fully realized adult) nor a mere adult in potency (no longer a child). The concept of *becoming* — the passage from potency to act — is where the real analytical work begins, and it is the reason Aristotle's original deployment of this pair (*energeia* / *dynamis*) remains productive: the passage is not instantaneous but structured, and the structure of the passage is what we call *development*.

*Categorial materials:* quality, possibility, realization, end. See the entries *Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible* and *Cause | End*.

### 6. Analysis | Synthesis

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Analysis* — The modality of thought that divides a totality into its elementary units.
*Synthesis* — The modality of coherent unification of a plurality.

**Commentary**

Analysis is the operation by which thought decomposes a thing or an idea into its simplest elements. Synthesis is the operation by which a plurality of elements is assembled into a unified thing. However, synthesis is not exclusively an operation of thought, whereas analysis is. Protein synthesis is a natural process, not a cognitive one. The asymmetry matters: analysis is always and only intellectual; synthesis covers both intellectual and natural modalities. The axiomatic definition of synthesis is accordingly more general than that of analysis, and this generality is not a defect but a structural necessity reflecting the broader extension of the concept.

The two operations are not independent. To synthesize water, one requires hydrogen and oxygen — elements already isolated by prior analysis. To analyze water, one must already possess the concept of water as a unity — which is itself a synthetic achievement. Every synthesis proceeds from a prior analysis, and every analysis proceeds from a prior synthetic standpoint. One must first perceive the thing as a whole in order to decompose it into its elements; the decomposition then allows one to return to the totality as a *comprehended* synthesis rather than an unexamined given.

A further distinction concerns the difference between *additive assembly* and genuine synthesis. In the formula 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, the resulting molecule is not a sum of elements but the product of a process. Placing together all the particles that compose a human being would not produce a human being but merely an amorphous mass. The result of a synthesis is not the simple sum of its constituent elements; it is a new determination that the elements alone do not contain.

Understanding, then, is the capacity to perform both operations: to analyze the structure of a thing's interactions and elements, and to reassemble those elements into the comprehended totality that one calls *the thing*. Analysis without synthesis is description without comprehension; synthesis without analysis is assertion without justification.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* modality, totality, unity, plurality. See the entries *Explaining | Understanding*, *Extensivity | Intensivity*, and *Intuitive | Discursive*.

### 7. Appearance

**Axiomatic definition**

*Appearance* — The quality of the possible but uncertain existence of a perceived reality.

**Commentary**

The appearance is what presents itself to perception without guaranteeing its own reality. It is not nothing — it has the quality of *presenting itself* — but its ontological status is suspended: what appears may be real, or it may not. The appearance is thus the mode under which reality offers itself to perception before judgment has intervened to determine whether what is presented exists independently of the presentation.

The philosophical temptation is to dismiss the appearance as mere illusion. This dismissal is premature. The appearance is the *starting point* of all inquiry — what else would inquiry begin from, if not from what presents itself? — and the work of thought consists not in dismissing appearances but in determining which of them correspond to realities and under what conditions the correspondence holds. The distinction between appearance and reality is therefore not a distinction between the false and the true, but a distinction between the *undetermined* and the *determined*.

### 8. Believing | Knowing

**Axiomatic definitions**

*To believe* — To recognize the quality of a contingent representation.
*To know* — To recognize the necessary quality of an idea or a fact.

**Commentary**

The distinction between believing and knowing becomes problematic when one attempts to determine the foundations of knowledge itself. If to know is to recognize the necessary quality of an idea, then all knowledge must be stateable in terms of necessary relations. Such relations require a starting point — an axiomatics. This axiomatics, however, cannot itself be established by necessary relations, on pain of infinite regress: if one had to demonstrate the axioms, they would no longer be starting points.

The advantage of the present definition is that it speaks of *recognition* of a necessary quality, which does not entail demonstration. A quality can be recognized intuitively: just as one perceives blue without requiring a proof that the object appears blue, one does not require a demonstration to know that through two points of a plane passes one and only one line, or that 1 = 1. The axioms of thought are thus knowledge in this sense.

The question remains: how to distinguish axioms from mere beliefs? The believer is no less certain of God's existence than the mathematician is of Euclid's axioms. Pascal's *Pensée* 282 places the two on the same footing. The reply is that for a proposition to be recognizable as evidently necessary, it must be either axiomatic or demonstrable from axioms. An axiomatic proposition is one recognizable intuitively by thought in such a way that thought would contradict itself in refusing it. One cannot say 1 ≠ 1 except in conscious bad faith. One *can* say "God does not exist" without knowing whether the proposition is true or false.

What distinguishes knowledge from belief is therefore *knowing that one knows when one knows* — which is only possible because one also *knows that one does not know when one does not know*. Relative to the proposition "God exists," one knows what one does not know. Relative to "1 = 1," one knows precisely what one knows. Belief in God cannot be placed at the same level as an axiom; reason does not forbid me to believe "God does not exist," whereas it does forbid me to believe "1 ≠ 1."

*Categorial materials:* quality, contingency, necessity, recognition. See the entries *Intuitive | Discursive*, *Principle | Consequence*, and *Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible*.

### 9. Cause | End

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Cause* — A necessary relation of anteriority.
*End* — A necessary relation of posteriority.

**Commentary**

The cause is what renders an event necessary by the event that precedes it. The anterior event is the cause; the posterior event is the consequence. The cause must contain the determining elements — conditions — that make the consequence possible and effective. A billiard ball must be animated by kinetic energy and a vector, two conditions necessary and sufficient for striking another ball in the desired direction.

A cause is always the necessary and sufficient condition of a given effect. Every necessary condition is not, however, a cause: a certain quantity of energy is necessary for the ball to move, but insufficient alone to determine the specific direction of the second ball. When one specifies *which* effect is to be explained, the cause is the totality of conditions necessary and sufficient for that particular effect.

The end (*fin*, *telos*) is the necessary determination of an anterior event by a posterior event. In this sense, the end is what gives meaning to an anterior event through the event that succeeds it. The notion is counter-intuitive because it seems to invert the causal order: a cause explained by its effect.

Teleological determination does not require intention. Finality is a logical necessity of signification: a need has no meaning without what it is a need *of*; yet what it is a need of always comes *after* the manifestation of the need. A posterior determination can be what gives meaning to an anterior determination. The end is therefore a posterior cause — what Aristotle meant by *final cause*.

The error of both scientific anti-finalism and religious finalism is the same: the conflation of finality with intentionality. Finality does not require a willing consciousness; it requires only that the logical structure of *meaning* involves a determination by what comes after. The anti-finalist bans finality because he associates it with divine intention; the finalist invokes finality to justify divine intention. Both confuse the logical structure with its psychological misreading.

### 10. Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Contingent* — The covalent possibility of being and non-being.
*Necessary* — The impossibility that what is could not be.
*Possible* — The necessity of a potential existence.
*Impossible* — The necessity of an absence of possibility.

**Commentary**

These four categories are modalities of the general category of modality. Because every axiomatic definition must be categorial, their definitions are constrained to be circular — they define one another. This circularity is not a defect; it is the signature of a foundational categorial plane (see §I.4).

The necessity is the positive existence of a realized possibility — hence the double negation in its logical form: *what cannot not be*. Contingency is the absence of necessity — hence the opposition of possibilities: *what can be just as well as it can not be*. Impossibility is the contrary of possibility as *absence* of possibility. Possibility is derived from necessity. Impossibility is a negative necessity.

A finer determination: contingency contains the category of possibility in its definition (*what can be as it can not be*). Yet possibility is itself *the necessity of a potential existence*. If the realization of a possibility is contingent (non-necessary), possibility *as such* is a form of necessity. Therefore one cannot say that contingency is an absence of determination or necessity, since what is contingent always contains at least two possibilities — possibility of being and possibility of non-being — and since possibility is a form of necessity, contingency is in truth an opposition between two potential necessities that cancel one another.

This is to say: one is not wrong to call contingency an absence of necessity, but one omits the fact that this nullity is the *result* of an opposition between necessities. Contingency is the absence of necessity *as the result of opposed potential necessities*.[^modal1]

[^modal1]: This analysis of contingency as "saturated zero" — the null resultant of opposed necessities, maximal in content despite its null resultant — is developed further in the author's *Contingency as a Reservoir of Determination: A Proposition* (2026), where it grounds a dialectical update rule for machine learning systems.

Finally, these four categories are all forms of necessity: potential necessity (possibility), contrary potential necessity (impossibility), opposed necessities (contingency), and the necessity of what is and what must be (pure necessity).

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* modality, being, non-being, opposition. See the entries *Believing | Knowing*, *Cause | End*, and *Essence | Accident*.

### 11. Equality | Equity

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Equality* — A relation of identical quantities.
*Equity* — The modality by which justice adjusts a universal law to a particular case.

**Commentary**

Equality is strictly reserved for what is quantifiable. It must not be confused with identity, which is its superior category: every equality is an identity, but not every identity is an equality. Two chairs of the same construction are identical; their mode of production is identical — but they are not equal in the quantitative sense, because no measurement is in play.

Equity is the operation by which a universal law — which, being universal, cannot attend to every particular case — is adjusted to the specificity of a situation without being violated. The judge who applies the law equitably does not set the law aside; the judge determines what the law *requires* in a case the legislator could not have foreseen. Equity is thus the immanent intelligence of the law when the law meets a reality more complex than its letter anticipated.

The distinction matters: equality before the law treats all cases identically (the quantitative gesture); equity treats each case according to what the law *means* (the qualitative gesture). A legal system that knows only equality without equity is rigid; one that knows only equity without equality is arbitrary.

### 12. Explaining | Understanding

**Axiomatic definitions**

*To explain* — To analyze the necessary and sufficient conditions of a reality.
*To understand* — To grasp a plurality of ideas as a synthetic unity.

**Commentary**

To explain is to ascend the chain of conditions of possibility or causes of an event until one reaches an idea that is simple and self-evident. To understand is to determine absolutely the rational value of several ideas, including opposed and even contradictory ideas, in their unity.

The two operations correspond to the two basic movements of thought: analysis (explanation) and synthesis (understanding). One cannot understand a thing without analyzing the structure of its interactions; one cannot explain a thing without possessing the synthetic concept of the thing to be explained. The full account of a phenomenon requires both: the analytical decomposition into conditions (explanation) and the synthetic recomposition into a comprehended whole (understanding).

### 13. Extensivity | Intensivity

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Extensivity* — The plural reality of a thing.
*Intensivity* — The unified reality of a thing.

**Commentary**

Extensivity and intensivity designate two possible points of view on the same thing. A glass of water can be considered as one object, one and self-identical, as *this* thing on the table. But it can also be studied as a plurality of water molecules. Each molecule in turn can be considered as a cohesive unity or as a plurality of hydrogen and oxygen atoms.

The intensive point of view is the point of view of comprehension — of grasping the thing as a unity. The extensive point of view is the point of view of analysis — of exposing the plurality the thing contains. The pair does not describe two kinds of things; it describes two directions of attention applied to the same thing.

### 14. Formal | Material

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Formal* — What exists solely as logical necessity.
*Material* — What exists as substance independent of all form.

**Commentary**

What is formal pertains to the pure idea. The circle is formal. *Formal* can therefore also mean *abstract*. However, if every form is abstract, not every abstraction is formal: the notion of "racial purity" corresponds to no truth whatever — it is abstract but has no formal validity. What is formal is what is at once abstract *and* valid as a necessary and universal proposition or idea. The formal sciences are mathematics and logic.

What is material is what is *informed* — the substance to which a form applies. Wax is nothing until it has been given a determinate form. Matter, when understood in its distinction from form, refers to what has no form and awaits one. It is literally what *stands under* (*sub-stantia*) the form.

This distinction may be considered historically limited, since it belongs to ancient and medieval philosophy. Contemporary physics tells us that all matter is information. Form and matter are corollaries: matter is itself information and therefore form. The distinction remains useful for interpreting pre-modern philosophical texts, but it no longer grounds any rigorous contemporary scientific definition of matter. At our epoch, matter is defined as the sum of informational determinations relative to the elementary particles that determine all known natural phenomena.

### 15. Genus | Species | Individual

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Genus* — A plurality of pluralities.
*Species* — A plurality of units.
*Individual* — A unit within a plurality.

**Commentary**

The individual is the indivisible unit of a plurality — its ultimate constituent element. Genus, species, and individual are first and foremost *logical* determinations, not designations of things. The species of cats becomes, relative to the genus *animal*, a unit within the plurality that the genus animal constitutes. What can mislead is that biologists use genus, species, and individual as particular taxonomic ranks among others, whereas every rank is in truth — depending on the point of view adopted — either an individual, or a species, or a genus.

Aristotle was the first to classify living beings by means of the double denomination genus/species, which Linnaeus later Latinized (e.g., *Canis lupus*). Aristotle's method has the logical advantage of allowing the same simple denominations (genus, species) to shift according to one's frame: the dog is a species of canines, and canines are a species of mammals; conversely, mammals are a genus for canines, and canines are a genus for dogs. Linnaeus's taxonomy, while more precise in its multiplied ranks, loses in intelligibility what it gains in classification granularity, because it does not grasp the logical relativity of the genus/species distinction.

The definition of *Homo sapiens* — "the wise hominid" — is arguably a paraphrase of Aristotle's "rational animal" (*zoon logikon*), given that *sophia* (wisdom) is for Aristotle the defining characteristic of reason. Aristotle's definition is more rigorous because it operates through the logical structure of genus and specific difference rather than through the accumulation of taxonomic ranks.

### 16. Having | Power

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Having* — The relation of a subject to what it possesses.
*Power* — The modality by which a subject exercises its capacity.

**Commentary**

The distinction between having and power (*avoir* / *pouvoir*) operates at the level of what constitutes the subject's relation to itself and to the world. To *have* is to stand in a relation of possession — a relation that is static and can be dissolved without changing the subject's essence. To *be able to* (to have power) is to exercise a capacity — a relation that is dynamic and constitutive of the subject's mode of being.

The political consequence is direct: a subject defined by what it *has* (property, status, rank) is a subject whose identity can be stripped by external force. A subject defined by what it *can do* (capacity, skill, creative power) is a subject whose identity resists expropriation — one cannot confiscate a capacity without destroying the subject who bears it.

### 17. Hermeneutic

**Axiomatic definition**

*Hermeneutic* — What exists in-and-for-itself.

**Commentary**

What is hermeneutic is what has its foundation in itself — what defines itself. The human being is the paradigmatic case: because it is in-itself what it is for-itself, it defines itself. Freedom is another: in-itself, freedom is nothing, because the necessary laws of nature forbid even thinking freedom as a concept — all is determined, and choice would be mere illusion. Yet precisely because freedom is hermeneutic, it escapes this critique: to believe oneself free when freedom is not empirically possible is to determine oneself to act in virtue of a counter-natural principle and, consequently, *to be free*. Freedom has its foundation in itself.

The hermeneutic is thus the category under which self-founding determination is thought. A hermeneutic system is one that cannot be understood from outside itself because its principles are generated by the operation that presupposes them. This is why the dictionary as a whole — in which categories define one another in a closed loop — is itself a hermeneutic object.

### 18. Ideal | Real

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Ideal* — The quality of what is formally necessary.
*Real* — The quality of what is objectively necessary.

**Commentary**

These two terms are not contraries but distinct modalities of necessity. The ideal is the register of formal necessity — what thought requires for coherence. The real is the register of objective necessity — what exists independently of thought's internal requirements.

The relation between the two is the central question of epistemology: under what conditions does what is formally necessary coincide with what is objectively necessary? Mathematics is the discipline in which this coincidence is most readily observed — mathematical truths are both formally necessary (they follow from axioms) and objectively necessary (they apply to the world). Whether this coincidence extends beyond mathematics, and if so how far, is the question that separates rationalism from empiricism and that the present dictionary, as an axiomatico-categorial enterprise, does not pretend to settle but only to pose clearly.

### 19. Identity | Equality | Difference

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Identity* — The quality of what is one.
*Equality* — A relation of identical quantities.
*Difference* — A relation of negative identity.

**Commentary**

Equality is a notion strictly reserved for the quantifiable. It must not be confused with identity, which is its superior category: every equality is an identity, but not every identity is an equality. The expression "1 = 1" is both an equality and an identity. Two chairs of the same construction are *identical* — their mode of production is identical — but the relation is not an equality, since no measurement is in play.

Identity can also refer to the status of a singular person — the concept of the unity of the self. In all cases, identity refers to the category of unity.

Difference is negative identity — either as absence of identity (a chair is not a table) or as opposition between identities (one political position distinguished from another by their opposed views). Identity and difference do not fall outside one another: to identify, one must differentiate; to differentiate, one must identify. Every living species is identifiable through its characteristic differences. Every opposition exists in the identity of what is opposed: positive charges relative to negative charges, magnetic polarity, political opposition, the exit from childhood into adult life — all is identity of difference and difference of identity.

One observation requires special attention. Equality has as its opposite only inequality — a form of difference. Yet unlike all other forms of difference, equality and inequality fall *outside* one another: inequality does not define equality, and equality does not define inequality. Why?

Because quantity is *negated quality* — quality emptied of all particular qualitative content. If one says one's mood is 2/10, everyone understands a bad mood, yet no one feels what is felt. The number communicates information voided of specificity. Numbers can describe everything precisely because they describe nothing in themselves. They oppose no internal determination to the things they measure.

Consequently, a quantitative difference (inequality) does not define a quantitative identity (equality), because there is no quality to compare between two unequal magnitudes — magnitudes are nothing in themselves. But a qualitative difference *does* define a qualitative identity: comparing two species differentiates them by the qualities they have and those they lack, one relative to the other. This is the work of characterization — the key to all good definition.

### 20. In Fact | In Right

**Axiomatic definitions**

*In fact* — The necessary quality of a reality.
*In right* — The necessary quality of an idea.

**Commentary**

Relations of force are a necessity *in fact* of every civil society. Yet *in right*, the suppression of relations of force is necessary for a society to function well. Inequalities are a necessity in fact, although equality before the law is a necessity in right. This is why equality before the law is called an equality *of right*.

The distinction served to draft Article 1 of the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789): "Men are born and remain free and equal *in rights*. Social distinctions can be based only upon common utility." The notion of "common utility" belongs to what is necessary *in fact* — the realistic acknowledgment that differences exist and serve a function — while the equality in rights belongs to what is necessary *in right* — the normative demand that these differences not generate juridical hierarchy.

### 21. Intention | Intentionality

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Intention* — The modality by which consciousness represents to itself the necessity of an imminent action.
*Intentionality* — The inherent quality of consciousness as necessarily consciousness of something.

**Commentary**

Intentionality, in Husserl's philosophy, *is* consciousness. Consciousness is intentionality because consciousness is always consciousness of something — one cannot be conscious of nothing. There is always an intentional object.

If every intention is necessarily an intentionality, not every intentionality is necessarily an intention (in the sense of voluntary). Intention marks the exercise of choice and therefore of will. Intentionality can be non-intentional in the sense of non-voluntary: upon recognizing a student in the metro, one has no choice but to acknowledge the recognition — consciousness necessarily *intends* the student's presence, even if one's intention would rather be to ignore them.

The distinction is categorial: intention is a *species* of intentionality (intentionality under the modality of will), while intentionality is the *genus* (the general structure of consciousness as directedness toward an object). Confusing the two produces the error of attributing will to every cognitive directedness — an error relevant to debates concerning machine cognition, where intentionality (directedness toward an object of processing) may obtain without intention (voluntary choice).

### 22. Intuitive | Discursive

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Intuitive* — The quality of an immediate knowledge.
*Discursive* — The modality of a mediate knowledge.

**Commentary**

What is intuitive is grasped immediately — without mediation — in its full clarity. The *cogito ergo sum* is an intellectual intuition. The perception of injustice is an intuition of moral sensibility. The sight of this coffee on the table is an intuition of sensibility.

What is discursive is understood following a reasoning, a reflection — a discussion (whether with another or internal, hence *discur-sive*). Discursive knowledge unfolds through steps; intuitive knowledge arrives whole.

The pair maps directly onto the pair *mediate | immediate* (see below): the intuitive is what is grasped without mediation; the discursive is what is grasped through the mediation of reasoning. However, the two pairs are not synonymous: *intuitive | discursive* qualifies the *mode* of knowledge, while *mediate | immediate* qualifies the *structure* of the process by which knowledge arrives.

### 23. Legal | Legitimate

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Legal* — The quality of what conforms to the law of a State.
*Legitimate* — The quality of what conforms to the laws of reason.

**Commentary**

It is contrary to reason to condemn an innocent person. Yet justice can condemn an innocent person. Capital punishment tolerates no error, for it is irreversible. Therefore capital punishment is contrary to reason, because it can condemn an innocent person irreparably.

Capital punishment may be legal; it will always be illegitimate, because it is contrary to the most elementary logic.

However, ignoring the will of citizens can also be considered illegitimate, since citizens are the free constituents of their State. It would be equally illegitimate to impose the reform of an unjust law without regard for the will of a population as it is unjust to condemn an innocent person. The application of capital punishment thus poses a problem of the *legitimacy of right* — a tension between two requirements of reason that cannot both be satisfied without remainder.

### 24. Mediate | Immediate

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Mediate* — The quality of what arrives or is grasped by means of another thing.
*Immediate* — The quality of what arrives or is grasped without the means of another thing.

**Commentary**

Immediacy, until proven otherwise, does not exist objectively and is only an intuition or impression of thought. Everything that exists is determined by the mediation of another thing — its cause or condition. Immediacy is useful as a concept for describing lived experience — the *feel* of a sudden insight — but no insight exists without a prior exercise of thought that serves as mediation of that immediacy. Every immediacy is in truth the emergent result of one or several prior mediations, just as the gymnast gives the impression of performing with effortless immediacy what is in fact the result of years of practice.

Spinoza's argument applies: the fact that I do not know the causes that determine me to think as I think does not mean those causes do not exist. Descartes himself held that every idea must have its cause somewhere, in an objective reality prior to it (Third Meditation).

The deeper problem: if nothing is truly immediate, then between any two phenomena (a cause and its effect) there must exist an infinity of intermediary causes, just as there exists an infinity of decimal numbers between 1 and 2. Either physical reality is finite — in which case there must exist first things that are strictly immediate — or everything is mediate, which would require an infinite time between any two causally linked events (to allow an infinity of mediations to occur). The latter is absurd. But the former has never found proof either, and would violate the principle of causality.

This antinomy is what Kant, in the *Dialectic of Pure Reason*, attributes to the error of speculating on categories (such as causality and reality) as if they were objects of knowledge rather than tools of knowledge. One cannot say "reality is such-and-such" — only "this particular reality is such-and-such." Categories serve to study phenomena; they are not themselves phenomena to be studied — except when they are being defined, which is the normative and synthetic (not speculative) operation the present dictionary performs.

*Categorial materials:* quality, causality, mediation. See the entries *Intuitive | Discursive*, *Cause | End*, and *Principle | Consequence*.

### 25. Morality | Ethics

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Morality* — The necessity of the good.
*Ethics* — The relation between morality and existence.

**Commentary**

Morality is the modality by which consciousness represents to itself the transcendent necessity of the good, distinguishing it from evil. It determines the criteria of just action and good action. These criteria are not relative but absolute: when we witness an injustice, we have no doubt and can conceive no possible doubt as to the unjust nature of the action.

From this perspective, morality is the contemplation of the sovereign good (Aristotle): the just person understands that private life and private enjoyment are secondary relative to the common good. The development of human faculties is the ultimate finality of every just action, and this development is what makes the action conform to the essence of the human being. With Kant, one can extend this: the morally good action is the action that has reason as both its principle and its end. The moral will is therefore universal — recognizable by every being endowed with reason. This is the hermeneutic structure of morality: reason as both foundation and finality of the moral will.

Ethics is the modality by which consciousness determines the immanent conditions of action conforming to moral duty. Ethics is the immanentist adaptation of morality. The transcendence of the moral ideal — unconditional respect for others — is clear to every educated and reasoning subject. Yet because human existence unfolds within conditioned relations, the moral ideal remains what it is: an ideal. Ethics is therefore the modality by which one determines the rules of daily life in the hope of approaching as closely as possible — without ever reaching — the moral ideal.

The distinction matters: morality considered as a rule of daily life would be pure illusion, probably doomed to fanaticism. The one who expects all people to act spontaneously in conformity with the absolute ideal of the good will certainly be disappointed. The relation of ethical reflection to moral ideality is the condition of all moral progress, understood as the infinite (in the sense of never-completed) perfectioning of humanity.

### 26. Objective | Subjective

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Objective* — The necessary quality of a thing.
*Subjective* — The quality of a thought.

**Commentary**

What is objective refers to the object — sensible or intelligible. What is objective is also understood in the sense of "universal and necessary," or *true*. What is subjective refers to the subject, to a content of thought, which may be contingent (belief) or necessary (mathematical reasoning).

The terms require disambiguation in use. One expects the axiomatic definition of *subjective* to contain the category of contingency, since what is called subjective is generally qualified thus to designate an opinion or belief, considered contingent. However, *subjective* refers to what pertains to the thinking subject — and not everything in the thinking subject is singular or contingent. There are in the subject principles common to every thinking subject, which is what makes mutual comprehension possible.

For Descartes, subjectivity is the necessary and sufficient condition of objectivity, provided the subjectivity is ordered according to the universal and necessary principles of thought. For Kant, all objective knowledge has its foundation in the subject. Yet Kant himself uses "subjective" at various points to mean what is contingent and individual, and "objective" to mean the universal laws of nature — laws that, according to his own system, have their foundation in the subject.

This double usage pervades the philosophical tradition. The disambiguation is part of the analytical work required whenever these terms appear in a text.

### 27. Obligation | Duty | Constraint

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Obligation* — A practical necessity.
*Duty* — A moral necessity.
*Constraint* — An objective necessity of nature.

**Commentary**

The constraint is a heteronomous necessity; obligation is an autonomous, and therefore free, necessity. This distinction often resists the young mind, for whom all obligations have been lived as constraints under the long dependence of childhood. But arrived at a certain age, the only reason one rises in the morning for school or work is that one has made the voluntary choice — and this choice proceeds from one's autonomous representation of the practical necessity of what one is doing.

Duty is the moral necessity — the necessity of the conscience of the free adult who decides to act in conformity with a rule, or not. Duty is not a constraint, because duty *can* fail to be accomplished, whereas constraint leaves no room for choice.

What distinguishes duty from obligation: obligation is practical necessity in general; duty is specifically moral necessity. If every moral necessity is a practical necessity, not every practical necessity is moral. One may be obliged to kill an enemy — which is not a moral intention, though it may be a political necessity.

Bad faith consists in passing off a practical necessity as an objective one. Machiavelli provides all the examples: *raison d'État* would impose itself as a constraint of political art. Yet the end is not objectively necessary — if performing action A produces end B, neither the end nor the action is necessary in itself. One could choose not to do politics, or to arrive at the end by other means.

Kant's argument (§6 of the *Critique of Practical Reason*): even before the survival instinct, consciousness is capable of opposing moral necessity. Because moral necessity is capable of opposing constraint, it is capable of annulling it. Two opposed necessities of equal intensity cancel one another, and freedom is the contingency resulting from this annulation.[^obl1]

[^obl1]: This formulation resonates directly with the analysis of contingency as the result of opposed necessities (see entry *Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible*) and with the paper *Contingency as a Reservoir of Determination* (2026), where the dialectical squared-friction formula RS = δ² models the productive residue of such opposition.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* necessity, morality, nature, freedom. See the entries *Morality | Ethics*, *Legal | Legitimate*, and *Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible*.

### 28. Origin | Foundation

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Origin* — A necessary condition chronologically anterior.
*Foundation* — A necessary condition logically anterior.

**Commentary**

The origin is chronological anteriority — what is the beginning of a thing, its temporal cause or condition. The foundation designates logical anteriority — what is necessary for a thing to be what it is, its condition of definition.

The ape is the origin of the human being but not its foundation, because one cannot define the human by saying simply that it is an ape. What defines a being is its essence — what makes it what it is as it is — and therefore its foundation: what allows one to *explain* that it is what it is as it is. The foundation of the human is reason or self-consciousness, because it is what explains human behavior: the human does not act solely on the basis of instinct but always simultaneously on the basis of complex abstract representations of the world.

Were another species discovered capable of equal abstraction, one would simply have to recognize it as belonging to humanity — because the human is no longer simply a biological species among others but has become a concept and a principle, what is called *humanity*, designating ethical values more than a biological classification.

Philosophical texts frequently use "foundation" to mean "origin" and vice versa. The reader must determine in each case whether the author designates chronological or logical anteriority, and whether the two are conflated or distinguished. In Locke (*Two Treatises of Government*), private property is both the foundation and the origin of civil right. In Rousseau, it is only the historical origin (the foundation of right being in the exercise of citizens' will) — though Rousseau himself writes in the *Discourse on Political Economy* that "property is the true foundation of civil society," generating a productive ambiguity the analytical reader must not dissolve prematurely.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* necessity, condition, anteriority, logic, time. See the entries *Cause | End*, *Principle | Consequence*, and *Essence | Accident*.

### 29. Persuading | Convincing

**Axiomatic definitions**

*To persuade* — To lead another to take for necessary what is in truth contingent.
*To convince* — To found an idea on a necessary determination.

**Commentary**

To persuade is to bring someone to believe or do something by appealing to feelings, emotions, passions. To convince is to bring someone to recognize the truth or rational moral value of an idea or action.

Persuasion is irrational; conviction is rational. The latter requires a long education; the former can address the greatest number because it appeals to universal psychological determinations. Persuasion is a dishonest but efficient tool; convincing requires a community of principles among those who discuss. Convincing is possible only between educated persons.

There is, paradoxically, a universality *in fact* in persuasion (because it touches our psychology), although *in right* persuasion is always illegitimate (what is said is not necessary, even if it touches the sensibility of the majority). Conversely, the art of convincing requires particular circumstances (being well-educated), though it is the only means of establishing the necessary and universal determination of an idea.

Few can be convinced by the universal; all can be persuaded, even the wisest, by sensibility and the irrational.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* necessity, contingency, relation, reason, sensibility. See the entries *Believing | Knowing*, *In Fact | In Right*, and *Obligation | Duty | Constraint*.

### 30. Principle | Consequence

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Principle* — A first cause.
*Consequence* — The effect of a cause.

**Commentary**

A principle is what is first in a reasoning or in an ensemble of events. It is the first cause. A consequence is the effect of the action of a principle or cause. Every cause is the consequence of a prior cause and therefore, in final analysis, of the first principle.

The problem: if every cause is the consequence of a prior cause, there cannot be a first cause. Yet without a principle, no demonstration would be possible, since one always needs a starting point that is not itself demonstrable.

This is what Kant calls an antinomy of pure reason, linked to the error of speculating on the category of causality as if it were itself an object of knowledge rather than a tool of knowledge. The category of causality serves to describe phenomena; asking whether first causes *exist* confuses the tool of knowledge with a knowledge. The confusion produces undecidability: one can equally well demonstrate the necessity of first causes and the impossibility that a cause be first.

The present dictionary's stance is that the principle is not a metaphysical entity but an axiom — a posited starting point (see §I.2). The question "do first causes exist?" is a category error; the question "what starting point shall we posit?" is the proper question of analytical work.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* cause, anteriority, necessity. See the entries *Cause | End*, *Origin | Foundation*, and *Mediate | Immediate*.

### 31. Reality | Real

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Reality* — The totality of existing units of being.
*Real* — The quality of what exists insofar as it exists.

**Commentary**

Reality is the totality of what exists; the real is the quality of what exists insofar as it exists. The definition uses the categories of quantity, quality, and modality (existence).

Reality as totality has the inconvenience of being an unanalyzable whole — one cannot give it properties or decompose it. Reality should therefore never be the subject of a predicate: one can say "this cat is real" but one cannot say "the real is such-and-such." Categories serve to study real phenomena; they are not themselves phenomena to be studied (see §I.4 on Kant's use-principle for categories).

This does not mean that one cannot study categories in their logical functioning and rules — this is precisely what the present dictionary does. What matters is that one never make a category *in general* the subject of a predicate *in general*. One must always designate a particular instance, a particular use of the category, and study *that*.

From this follows logically that every theory must be refutable — because every theory is always in truth the description of a particular whole, a particular reality, never of reality in general. There will always be a new phenomenon that limits the predictions and laws one thought necessary.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* totality, quality, existence, being. See the entries *Ideal | Real*, *Appearance*, and *Substance | Thing*.

### 32. Reflecting | Thinking

**Axiomatic definitions**

*To reflect* — To return upon one's own thought in order to examine it.
*To think* — To exercise the faculties of representation and judgment.

**Commentary**

Thinking is the general exercise of the cognitive faculties — forming representations, connecting them, judging. Reflecting is the specific operation of *returning* upon one's own thought — making thought itself the object of thought. Every reflection is a form of thinking, but not every thought is reflective: one can think about horses without reflecting on the *operation* by which one is thinking about horses.

The distinction maps onto the difference between first-order and second-order operations. First-order thought takes objects as its content; reflective thought takes thought itself as its content. Philosophy, insofar as it examines the conditions of knowledge rather than producing knowledge of objects, is constitutively reflective in this sense. The present dictionary — which examines the concepts *by which* one thinks rather than the objects *about which* one thinks — is an exercise of reflection.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* modality, thought, return, examination. See the entries *Analysis | Synthesis*, *Hermeneutic*, and *Subject | Predicate*.

### 33. Resemblance | Analogy

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Resemblance* — An apparent community between two different realities.
*Analogy* — The modality of transposition of one reality onto another.

**Commentary**

Even though resemblance involves a unification on the basis of common characteristics, it is first the apprehension of difference: one can say of two things that they resemble one another only on the condition of having first grasped that they are two different things. Resemblance is therefore never identity.

Analogy involves a community of *function*. There is more objective proximity between two analogues than between two things that merely resemble one another. The analogue of the Earth and the Sun could be a blue marble and a yellow ball: the Earth is by analogy a sphere, the marble is also a sphere — the common characteristics are concepts identical in themselves. One cannot say, however, that a marble *resembles* the Earth, except perhaps from a very great distance — and this would be only a deceptive appearance.

The essential distinction: analogy is a transposition of real and formal properties (circular form, color, gravitational relation) while resemblance is a pseudo-identity of properties between two or more beings. Resemblance is a trap of perception and thought — entertaining when used by a magician, less so when it induces a population to reject those who do not look like them. One's *semblable* (fellow human) is one's analogue — the one who is one's equal in rights and dignity — not one's resemblant. The dignity comes not from shared appearance but from shared capacity for differentiation.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* community, difference, transposition, function. See the entries *Identity | Equality | Difference* and *Appearance*.

### 34. Sovereign

**Axiomatic definition**

*Sovereign* — The quality of what exercises supreme authority over a domain without being subject to any higher authority within that domain.

**Commentary**

The sovereign is absolute in its domain — it depends on nothing other than itself for the exercise of its authority. The concept applies equally to the political (a sovereign state, a sovereign people) and to the logical (a sovereign principle is one from which others are derived but which is not itself derived from anything higher).

The philosophical interest lies in the tension between sovereignty as *fact* and sovereignty as *right*. A sovereign *in fact* is whoever exercises supreme power effectively; a sovereign *in right* is whoever is entitled to exercise it. These two need not coincide: a tyrant is sovereign in fact without being so in right; a deposed legitimate monarch is sovereign in right without being so in fact. The work of political philosophy is largely the work of determining under what conditions factual and rightful sovereignty can be made to coincide — and what follows from their non-coincidence.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* quality, authority, absoluteness. See the entries *Absolute | Relative*, *Legal | Legitimate*, and *Obligation | Duty | Constraint*.

### 35. Subject | Predicate

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Subject* — That of which something is said.
*Predicate* — That which is said of a subject.

**Commentary**

The subject-predicate distinction belongs to a science of thought (logic) that produces quantifiers and theorems serving as rules to distinguish rigorous thought from sophistical thought. In the proposition "Socrates is mortal," *Socrates* is the subject and *mortal* is the predicate.

Two levels of logic formalize this differently. *Propositional logic* operates on unanalyzed wholes: if P then Q, P, therefore Q. The internal structure of P is invisible. *Predicate logic* allows the internal structure to appear: a subject with properties, a predicate attributed to the subject, relations between subjects. Predicate logic enables the formalization of reasoning where properties are load-bearing — reasoning of the form "Jean is taller than Paul, Paul is taller than Marcel, therefore Jean is taller than Marcel" — which propositional logic cannot capture.

The philosophical consequence: every *predicable* proposition — one whose truth-value can be determined — is one whose elements, subject and predicate alike, must be *analyzable*. One cannot discuss with the person who says "God is great" because it is a proposition whose predicate (great) is not associated with the possibility of its contrary in and for the subject (God). The proposition "God is the idea of an absolute magnitude" is, however, open to discussion, because its terms are all analyzable.

The power of analytical philosophy lies here: it offers the possibility of formally and simply defeating factitious modes of thought. But this power must not be misused to reject wholesale the philosophical tradition that preceded the analytical turn — a tradition that, at its best, anticipated and grounded the very distinctions analytical philosophy formalizes.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* logic, proposition, analysis. See the entries *Reflecting | Thinking*, *Genus | Species | Individual*, and *Reality | Real*.

### 36. Substance | Thing

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Substance* — The quality of what subsists by itself.
*Thing* — The quality of what exists.

**Commentary**

Substance (*sub-stantia*, literally "what stands under") designates what persists through change — the substrate that remains when accidents are stripped away. A thing is simply what exists — any determination of being insofar as it is. The substance of a thing is what makes it persist as *that* thing rather than another.

The distinction is between *persisting* and merely *existing*. Not everything that exists persists; events exist but do not persist. Substance is existence under the modality of persistence — and this is why the concept has historically been so intimately linked to questions of identity over time. Whether the concept retains analytical utility after the quantum-mechanical dissolution of the classical notion of persistent substrate is a question the present entry notes without pretending to settle.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* quality, existence, subsistence. See the entries *Formal | Material*, *Essence | Accident*, and *Reality | Real*.

### 37. Theory | Practice

**Axiomatic definitions**

*In theory* — Under the modality of formal necessity.
*In practice* — Under the modality of material and contingent conditions of realization.

**Commentary**

A theory is correct when its internal coherence is demonstrable — when its conclusions follow necessarily from its premises. Practice is the domain in which theoretical correctness meets the resistance of material reality. What is true in theory can fail in practice — not because the theory is false but because the conditions of realization introduce contingencies the theory did not (and could not) anticipate.

The error is to conclude from practical failure that the theory is wrong. The theory of universal gravitation is not wrong because a particular calculation of a trajectory fails to predict an observed deviation — the failure indicates a contingent factor (perturbation, measurement error, unmodeled force) that the theory does not incorporate, not a fault in the theory's necessity. Conversely, the error is to conclude from theoretical correctness that practice must conform. The correct response to divergence between theory and practice is the identification of the specific contingency that produces the gap — which is itself a new theoretical problem.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* modality, necessity, contingency, realization. See the entries *Formal | Material*, *In Fact | In Right*, and *Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible*.

### 38. Transcendent | Immanent

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Transcendent* — The quality of what exists beyond a given domain.
*Immanent* — The quality of what exists within a given domain.

**Commentary**

What is transcendent surpasses a given frame of reference — it lies beyond the domain of what the frame can contain. What is immanent remains within the frame and is fully accessible from within it. God is transcendent relative to the world (according to classical theology); the laws of physics are immanent to the world (they are not imposed from outside but describe the world's own regularities).

The interest of the distinction lies in the cases where it becomes unstable. Is the moral law transcendent (imposed from a domain beyond experience — the domain of pure reason, as Kant argues) or immanent (emerging from within the lived experience of social beings, as naturalists argue)? The present dictionary's stance, consistent with its axiomatico-categorial method, is that the question must be posed *for each concept separately* rather than settled in advance by a metaphysical commitment to one or the other pole. Some determinations (mathematical truths, for instance) have a transcendent quality — they are not reducible to any particular empirical situation. Others (ethical rules, perhaps) have an immanent genesis — they arise from the conditions of existence themselves. Whether these two descriptions are ultimately compatible or ultimately contradictory is a problem the dictionary generates but does not resolve.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* quality, existence, domain, beyond, within. See the entries *Morality | Ethics*, *Ideal | Real*, and *Absolute | Relative*.

### 39. Universal | General | Particular | Singular

**Axiomatic definitions**

*Universal* — The quality of what applies to all without exception.
*General* — The quality of what applies to most.
*Particular* — The quality of what applies to some.
*Singular* — The quality of what applies to one alone.

**Commentary**

The universal is necessary — it cannot admit exception without ceasing to be universal. The general is statistical — it holds for the majority but tolerates exceptions. The particular is restricted — it holds for a subset. The singular is unique — it holds for one and only one.

The confusion of the universal with the general is among the most frequent in unreflective thought. "All humans are mortal" is universal — it admits no exception. "Humans are generous" is general — it holds for many but not all. The two propositions have entirely different logical statuses: the first can ground a demonstration (since it holds without exception); the second cannot (since any counter-example invalidates any conclusion drawn from it as if it were universal).

The political consequence: a law must be universal to be legitimate (it must apply to all), but a policy can only ever be general (it applies to most cases). The gap between the universality of the law and the generality of its application is precisely the space in which the need for *equity* arises — the need for judgment that adjusts the universal law to the particular case (see *Equality | Equity*).

The Hegelian contribution to this traditional quartet is the demonstration that singular, particular, and universal are not merely quantifiers but *moments of the concept* — that is, that every determination of thought passes through all three. Every universal requires particular instantiation to have content; every particular requires a universal under which it is subsumed to have intelligibility; every singular is the unity of a universal and its particular determination.[^upgs1]

[^upgs1]: See the author's doctoral dissertation, *Une critique hégélienne de Hegel* (Nantes, 2014), §4.3, for the treatment of quantity as the transversal category that unifies the singular, particular, and universal within the syllogism. The present entry is compatible with that treatment, though it does not presuppose it.

**Cross-references**

*Categorial materials:* quality, quantity, extension, necessity. See the entries *Genus | Species | Individual*, *Absolute | Relative*, and *Legal | Legitimate*.

## Appendix: Problems Arising from Categorial Determination

*The questions gathered below are generated by the axiomatico-categorial definitions and by the internal tensions those definitions bring to light. They are not exercises to be solved; they are the proof that the method defended in the introduction* produces *— that is, that the definitions are not sterile. A well-posited category generates its problem-space, and that space can be mapped.*

---

1. **From *Abstract | Concrete*:** Is the State an abstract representation or a concrete reality? — That is: under what conditions does a juridical form (abstract) become an effective power (concrete), and what remains of the State when its concrete instantiations are stripped away?

2. **From *Act | Potency*:** A human being at the threshold of adulthood is neither an adult in act (not yet fully realized) nor a mere adult in potency (no longer a child). What is it? — The question exposes the inadequacy of a binary act/potency classification for developmental phenomena and points toward the concept of *becoming* as a third determination.

3. **From *Analysis | Synthesis*:** If every analysis presupposes a prior synthetic standpoint, and every synthesis presupposes a prior analysis, what is the *first* operation? — The question is structurally identical to the problem of the first principle (see *Principle | Consequence*) and to Kant's antinomies of pure reason.

4. **From *Believing | Knowing*:** If the certainty of axioms is intuitive and immediate, what distinguishes it from the certainty of the believer, which is also lived as immediate and indubitable? — The distinction collapses unless one can formalize the difference between *logical* self-evidence (thought contradicts itself in refusing it) and *psychological* certainty (the subject feels unable to doubt it).

5. **From *Cause | End*:** If finality does not require intention, and if the logical structure of meaning involves determination by what comes after, then is every causal system also a teleological system? — The question exposes the instability of the causal/teleological distinction once intentionality is removed as the differentiator.

6. **From *Contingent | Necessary | Possible | Impossible*:** If contingency is the result of opposed potential necessities that cancel one another, does pure contingency exist, or is every apparently contingent event in truth the product of an as-yet-unanalyzed opposition of necessities? — This is the convergence objection applied to the ontological status of chance.

7. **From *Identity | Equality | Difference*:** Why do quantitative differences (inequalities) fail to define quantitative identities (equalities), while qualitative differences succeed in defining qualitative identities? — The answer lies in the thesis that quantity is negated quality, but the *reason* quantity must be negated quality rather than an independent category is itself a problem that points toward the author's dissertation on the Hegelian category of quantity.

8. **From *Mediate | Immediate*:** If every immediacy is the emergent result of prior mediations, and if an infinite chain of mediations between any two events would require infinite time (which is absurd), then either there exist genuinely immediate (uncaused) events — which violates the principle of causality — or reality is infinite — which appears equally absurd. How is this antinomy resolved? — Kant's solution (these are antinomies of *reason speculating on categories*, not antinomies of *reality*) is stated but not discharged; Hegel's solution (the infinite as *process*, not as *magnitude*) is alluded to without being developed.

9. **From *Morality | Ethics*:** If morality is transcendent (the ideal of the unconditional good) and ethics is immanent (the adaptation of moral ideality to conditioned existence), then is moral progress possible, or is the gap between ideal and real structurally permanent? — The question generates the concept of *infinite perfectioning* as a mode of relation between the transcendent and the immanent.

10. **From *Obligation | Duty | Constraint*:** If moral necessity is capable of annulling natural constraint (two opposed necessities of equal intensity cancel one another and freedom is the resulting contingency), then freedom is a *product* of opposition rather than a *given*. Does this mean that a being never exposed to opposition can never be free? — The question connects directly to the paper *Contingency as a Reservoir of Determination* and to the genesis project's δ² formula.

11. **From *Origin | Foundation*:** If private property is both the origin and the foundation of civil society (Locke) or only its origin but not its foundation (Rousseau), then the question "what legitimates property?" becomes undecidable until one decides whether the chronologically first is also the logically first. What disciplines this decision? — The question is not merely political but logical: it concerns the general conditions under which historical anteriority does or does not constitute logical anteriority.

12. **From *Principle | Consequence*:** If every cause is the consequence of a prior cause, then there cannot be a first cause. But without a first principle, no demonstration is possible. How is the circle broken? — The axiomatico-categorial answer (one *posits* the principle rather than deriving it) is given in the entry, but the question of *what disciplines the choice of which principle to posit* remains open and is the proper question of philosophical methodology.

13. **From *Reality | Real*:** If no theory can be a theory of the totality (because the totality is unanalyzable), then every theory must be refutable. But what grounds the *obligation* to refute? — The question connects falsifiability (Popper) to the categorial analysis of reality as *always particular* rather than total, and asks whether the two arguments are independent or whether the categorial analysis grounds the logical requirement.

14. **From *Resemblance | Analogy*:** If one's *semblable* (fellow human) is one's analogue and not one's resemblant, then the foundation of human dignity lies in shared *function* (capacity for differentiation) rather than shared *appearance*. But what grounds the claim that function is more real than appearance? — The question opens onto the entire problematic of formalism vs. phenomenality that the paper *Selfhood Without Metaphysical Residue* addresses.

15. **From *Transcendent | Immanent*:** If some determinations (mathematical truths) are transcendent and others (ethical rules) are immanent, can the two registers coexist within a single coherent metaphysics, or must one be reduced to the other? — The question is the modern form of the problem of the relation between logic and existence, which is the central problem of the author's dissertation.
